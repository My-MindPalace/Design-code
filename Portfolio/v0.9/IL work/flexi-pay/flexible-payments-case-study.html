01 — Overview

What we built & why it mattered

itilite is a B2B travel and expense management platform used by mid-to-large enterprises. The Flexible Payment Methods feature gave finance admins a dedicated console to manage corporate cards, assign them to teams, and control employee payment behaviour.

🏦

Corporate Card Management

Admins can add multiple corporate cards — Visa, Mastercard, Amex — and assign each to specific employees or teams. Cards are stored securely and surfaced contextually during booking.

⚡

Priority & Access Control

Each card can be toggled ON/OFF and marked as "Prioritized," controlling which card gets surfaced first during the expense flow. Granular team-level scoping is supported.

🧑‍💼

Employee Permission Layer

A separate control for whether employees are allowed to use their own personal cards at all — critical for companies that want to enforce 100% corporate card usage.

📊

Zero-State First Design

The interface was designed from the ground up with the empty state as the entry point — making onboarding frictionless and guiding admins to add their first card with clear, minimal CTAs.

02 — Problem

The control gap that was costing companies

Finance teams at enterprises had no structured way to enforce card policies across employees. Employees would book travel on personal cards by default, or use the wrong corporate card, creating reconciliation nightmares.

"We have 5 corporate cards for different departments, but employees just use whatever they feel like. By the time expenses come in, we have no idea which card was supposed to be used."

— Finance Admin, enterprise itilite customer
01

No team-level card assignment

Cards weren't scoped to teams. A marketing card could be used by engineering — creating messy expense reports and budget overruns.

02

Personal card defaults

Without explicit controls, employees defaulted to personal cards — defeating the purpose of having corporate cards entirely.

03

No admin visibility

Finance had no single view of all cards, who could use them, and whether they were active — forcing them to rely on spreadsheets and Slack.

05 — Design Decisions

The thinking behind the choices

Every component in this feature was the result of deliberate trade-offs — choosing simplicity and confidence over optionality.

01

Nickname over card number as primary identity

In the table view, the card nickname (not the issuing bank or network) is the primary label. Finance admins mentally organize cards by purpose ("Marketing card"), not by number. This aligns the UI with their mental model.

Visa 8342 Marketing_card ••••8342
02

Toggle over dropdown for Prioritized status

A toggle is a two-state switch — ON or OFF. A dropdown would suggest multiple states or rankings. Since priority in this context is binary (show first or don't), the toggle is semantically correct and more scannable in a table.

Priority: High ▾ Prioritized · ON ⬤
03

Empty state as an onboarding surface

Rather than showing a blank table with an "Add" button in a toolbar, the zero state takes over the full content area — making the action impossible to miss. The secondary action ("Who can use their own cards?") surfaces a critical policy setting early.

Empty table row Full zero-state card
04

Modal for add-card vs. full-page form

Using a modal keeps the admin in context — they can see the existing card list behind the overlay, which prevents the feeling of "navigating away." The form is short enough to not require a full page, and the task has a clear, contained scope.

Full-page form Focused modal overlay

07 — Learnings

What I'd do differently

01

Bulk card assignment would have helped enterprise rollout

Several enterprise admins during testing had 15–20 cards to configure. The one-by-one modal flow, while clean, became tedious at scale. A CSV import or multi-card onboarding wizard would have dramatically improved adoption for large accounts.

02

The "Prioritized" concept needed better education

A small but consistent pattern in user testing: admins toggled "Prioritized" ON for all cards, not understanding it was a relative ranking. An in-line tooltip or ordering metaphor (drag to reorder) would have surfaced the intent more clearly.

03

The employee-side of the story deserved more exploration

Most design effort went into the admin console. But the point of failure — employees using personal cards — lived in the booking flow, not admin settings. A tighter connection between the admin configuration and what employees actually saw at checkout would have closed the loop faster.
